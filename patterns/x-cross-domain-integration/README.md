# Pattern X — Cross-Domain Integration Principle

*Centre-recursive cross-referencing. Integration as structural property of the architecture, not editorial gesture.*

Full treatment: [`METHODOLOGY.md` § X](../../METHODOLOGY.md). This pattern is a writing discipline; no code component implements it.

## Problem It Solves

Integral knowledge systems claim that everything connects. But *demonstrating* connection in prose, without forcing it, is a craft problem most integral writing fails to solve. The typical failure mode is the parenthetical gesture: a health article that mentions consciousness in a footnote, an economics essay that nods toward ecology in the conclusion, a meditation guide that acknowledges the body in passing. These gestures signal awareness of integration without achieving it. The connections are decorative rather than structural.

## The Solution in Brief

The fractal topology (Pattern I) and the centre-spoke topology (Pattern II) together provide the structural basis for genuine cross-domain integration. Because every sub-wheel's centre is a fractal of the master centre, and because every spoke connects back to its sub-wheel centre, *the architecture itself generates the connections*.

A health article naturally touches consciousness because the centre of the Wheel of Health (in Harmonism: *Monitor* — sovereign diagnostic awareness) is a fractal of *Presence*. A service article naturally touches relationships because the centre of Service (*Dharma*) connects to the centre of Relationships (*Love*) through the master centre. The connections are not imposed by editorial policy — they are **generated by the architecture**.

## The Craft Move

The craft of cross-domain writing, then, is not inventing connections but *following the ones the architecture reveals*. When writing about sleep, the connection to consciousness is not a decorative aside — it is structural: sleep is governed by circadian biology (Health pillar), but sleep quality is profoundly affected by the state of consciousness at the transition into sleep (Presence centre), and the dreams emerging during sleep are a legitimate domain of learning (Learning pillar) and self-knowledge (Presence again).

The article does not need to mention all of these — but it should be *written from within an architecture where these connections are visible*, so that the reader ready to follow any thread finds the wikilink waiting.

## Adoption Path

1. Build the topology first (Pattern I) — without the structure, there are no structural connections to follow.
2. Identify each spoke's sub-wheel centre, and the fractal correspondence between sub-wheel centre and master centre.
3. When writing within any pillar, ask: *what connections does the architecture make available for this subject?* Wikilink the most load-bearing one or two, leave the others implicit.
4. Resist the parenthetical gesture. If a connection is real, make it a wikilink the reader can follow; if it isn't structural, don't mention it at all.

## The Anti-Pattern

The most common failure: the editorial mandate to "always mention other domains" or "always close with a paragraph on integration." This produces what it tries to prevent — performative integration that signals integration-as-virtue while breaking the rhythm of the actual subject. Integration is structural or it is nothing.

## Status

Doctrine-grade. The pattern is a writing discipline grounded in the structural patterns (I and II). Adoption is editorial; the architecture must already exist for the discipline to be practicable.
